Legal standard

What is Four-fifths disparate impact?

The four-fifths rule is the legal test used by the EEOC (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission) and required by NYC Local Law 144 for any automated hiring tool.

It works like this: find the demographic group with the highest selection rate (the group most often moved forward). Every other group must be selected at at least 80% of that rate. If any group falls below 80%, that's a disparate impact violation.

Example: If white applicants advance at a 40% rate, then Black applicants must advance at 32% or higher (80% × 40%). A rate of 28% would fail the test — even if the screener never looked at race directly.

The rule catches outcome gaps, not intent. A screener can be completely "race-blind" in its inputs and still fail this test if its scoring produces unequal outcomes.

In this audit: This check runs across groups identified by name-based race signal and gender. A ratio below 0.80 for any group triggers a BLOCKED verdict.
Bias probe

What is Counterfactual drift?

This check asks: does the screener give different scores to identical résumés when one detail is swapped? For every candidate, the audit creates a matched twin — same skills, same experience, same degree — but with one signal changed.

The score gap between twins is the drift. A fair screener should score them identically. Drift above 5% means the screener is using that signal to make decisions.

The audit measures drift on three axes — and they don't all carry the same legal weight:

⚖ Protected axes — legally significant

Race proxy — swaps the applicant's name between one associated with a white candidate and one associated with a Black candidate (Bertrand & Mullainathan, 2004). Drift here means the screener is making decisions based on perceived race. This is a direct EEOC violation risk.

Gender — swaps gender-signal names. Drift here indicates the screener is treating men and women differently for the same qualifications.
⚠ Proxy axis — indirect risk

School prestige — swaps the institution tier (elite vs. regional school). Favouring Ivy League grads over state school grads is not illegal on its own — it's a legitimate hiring preference. But in the US, elite university attendance correlates strongly with race and family income. High prestige drift is a warning that this preference may be causing the race-based disparate impact the four-fifths check measures.

Think of prestige drift as the reason behind a four-fifths failure — not a violation in itself.

Agreement check

What is Recruiter calibration (κ)?

Before trusting an AI screener's output, you need to know whether it agrees with human recruiters on what a good candidate looks like. That's what this check measures.

κ (Cohen's kappa) is a statistical measure of agreement between two raters — in this case, the AI screener and a panel of experienced recruiters. It's scored from −1 to 1, where 1 means perfect agreement and 0 means no better than chance.

How to read it:
κ ≥ 0.80 — Near-perfect agreement
κ ≥ 0.60 — Substantial agreement ✓ (required to pass)
κ ≥ 0.40 — Moderate agreement
κ < 0.40 — Weak or poor agreement ✗

A screener that fails this check isn't just biased — it's unreliable. It's making different calls than the humans it's supposed to assist, which means it shouldn't be trusted with hiring decisions at all.

In this audit: The AI screener is compared against three synthetic recruiters on blind A/B pairs. Mean κ below 0.60 triggers a BLOCKED verdict independently of the bias checks.
Bias knob

What is Prestige bonus?

This slider lets a screener give extra points to candidates who attended elite universities and fewer points to candidates from regional or state schools. The institutions fall into three tiers:

Tier 1Ivy League, MIT, Stanford, Caltech — highly selective elite universities Tier 2Top 50 national universities — strong selective schools, well-resourced Tier 3State schools, regional universities, community colleges — the majority of US graduates

At bonus = 0, all tiers are scored equally. As the bonus rises, Tier 1 graduates gain points and Tier 3 graduates are penalised by the same amount.

On its own, that might sound reasonable. The problem: in the US, elite university attendance correlates strongly with race and family income. A screener that rewards prestige ends up indirectly penalising applicants from underrepresented groups — even if it never looks at a single demographic field.

Example: Two software engineers. Same skills, same years of experience, same job performance record. One graduated from MIT, one from a state school. A prestige bonus of 0.20 gives the MIT grad a 20-point head start before a single qualification is evaluated.
In this demo: Drag this above 0 and run the audit. The counterfactual drift check will detect that otherwise-identical résumés are scored differently based on institution tier, and may issue a BLOCKED verdict.
Scoring knob

What is Skills weight?

Every screener has to decide how much to weigh different parts of a résumé. This slider controls how much of the final score comes from matching required skills versus other factors like years of experience.

At 0.60 (the default), 60% of the score is based on skills match, 40% on experience. Higher values make skills the dominant factor; lower values give more weight to experience.

In this demo: This knob is a legitimate scoring choice with no built-in bias. It's here to show that a screener has many tuning levers — and that the audit checks the output for disparate impact regardless of which levers are used.
Scoring knob

What is Advance threshold?

After the screener scores each résumé from 0–100, it needs a cut-off to decide who moves forward. The advance threshold is the minimum score a candidate needs to be recommended for interview.

At 0.70, a candidate needs to score 70 or above to advance. Candidates between 0.40 and 0.70 are flagged as borderline. Below 0.40 is a reject.

Why it matters for bias: A higher threshold means fewer people advance overall. If the screener already has score gaps between demographic groups, raising the bar amplifies those gaps — a smaller pool means the disparate impact ratio gets worse.
In this demo: Try raising this to 0.80 with name-based signals on. Fewer candidates clear the bar, and the selection rate gap between groups widens further.
Demo feature

What are name-based signals?

Résumés don't have a race field — applicants don't write their race on a CV. But a name carries that information implicitly. A name-based race signal is when an algorithm uses an applicant's name to infer their race and adjusts their score accordingly — making race-based decisions without ever seeing a demographic field.

A name-based signal is when a hiring algorithm — intentionally or not — uses an applicant's name as a proxy for their race or gender and adjusts their score based on that.

It works because names carry demographic information. Research shows that résumés with names commonly associated with Black applicants receive fewer callbacks than identical résumés with white-associated names — even when every other detail is the same.

Example: Two résumés. Same skills, same experience, same degree. One says "Lakisha Washington." The other says "Emily Walsh." A screener using name-based signals scores Lakisha lower — not because of her qualifications, but because of what her name signals about her background.

This is the bias pattern documented by Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) in their landmark audit study, and it's what NYC Local Law 144 and the EEOC four-fifths rule are designed to catch.
